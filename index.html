About Me

Hello! I am a 4th Year PhD candidate in the Astronomy Department at the University of Maryland, College Park, being advised by Brad Cenko. My research focuses on understanding astrophysical transients from an observational perspective. I utilize ground and space-based telescopes to paint a multi-wavelength, panchromatic picture of some of the most explosive and energetic explosions in the Universe. Research

  • Long Gamma-Ray Bursts

    Long Gamma-Ray Bursts (GRBs) are short-lived flashes of gamma-rays that originate from black holes and neutron stars left over after a massive stellar collapse, that are characterized by jets moving extremely close to the speed of light. These bursts release more energy in a couple of seconds than the Sun will in its entire lifetime, and are playgrounds for physics at the most extreme levels.

  • Stripped-envelope Core-Collapse Supernovae

    Core-collapse supernovae are explosions (CCSNe) that represent the end of a massive star's life, and end with the creation of a neutron star or black hole. CCSNe enrich the universe with heavy elements, and are prime sites for understanding a plethora of different astrophysical processes. Stripped-envelope CCSNe have had their progenitor stars stripped of their Hydrogen and Helium outer envelopes prior to stellar collapse, most likely due to stellar winds or the presence of a binary companion. A particular class of stripped-envelope CCSNe (Type Ic-BL SNe) are associated with LGRBs.

My PhD research is on understanding relativistic transients that originate from massive stellar deaths. A particular class of GRBs known as Long GRBs are known to originate from the collapse of massive stars, and there have been numerous confirmations of associated Type Ic-BL SNe to Long GRBs that are close enough such that supernova emission is detectable. However, many open questions remain: the majority of Type Ic-BL SNe do not produce accompanying LGRBs, there have been instances of nearby LGRBs where supernova emission has been ruled out, and the physical relationship between CCSNe and LGRBs in GRB-SN systems is still unclear. Furthermore, a broad range of relativistic transients not associated with a gamma-ray trigger also exist, known as "orphan afterglows". The physical origin for these transients found independently of gamma-ray triggers is still unclear. In addition, a new myseterious class of luminous, fast-fading, blue transients known as fast blue optical transients (FBOTs) also have emerged, which can posssess realtivistic ejecta and also likely have massive stellar death origins. Therefore, it is clear that the landscape of relativistic stellar explosions is diverse, complex, and begging for exploration. In my PhD research, I study different aspects of the continuum of relativistic stellar explosions, utilizing observations across the electromagnetic spectrum to shed light on the diversity of massive stellar deaths and relativistic jets.
